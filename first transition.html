The Balcony — Public Health Leadership Compass · Part One

You were exceptionally good
at holding it all together.
That's what makes this hard.

Think about how you actually learned to do what you do as an operational lead, and the skills you gained. Not just the technical and clinical skills — though those are real and hard-won — but the operational intelligence. You learned how responsive health work moves. You knew which situations were about to escalate before they did. You could hold multiple concurrent responses in your head, prioritise without being told, cover gaps before they became failures, and keep the workflow moving under pressure that most people outside the field wouldn't fully understand.

You became someone the team looked to — because you genuinely knew what to do when things were unpredictable. That knowledge wasn't given to you. You built it, over years, by being in it.

You held onto that operational role not only because you were good at it, but because you had to. The workforce wasn't always there. There were real gaps in real work that you had to fill. That was you responding to the actual conditions you were working in.

The problem is that necessity and identity become fused over time — and when the conditions finally change, the habit and the self-concept don't automatically change with them.

When a new leadership role arrives without sufficient scaffolding for a new identity to form — without a clear transition, without mentoring, without someone naming what the role actually requires — something predictable happens. A person defaults to what feels solid and certain. And the operational work is exactly that: visible, immediate, legible. You do it, and you can see whether it worked. Leadership is slower, less visible, and harder to feel confident about, especially when no one has helped you understand what it looks like from the inside.

So the operational role becomes the place where you feel genuinely capable and legitimately useful — while the leadership role sits alongside it, held in title, known but not quite inhabited. This very human response to uncertainty is extremely common in pressured health and other responsive operational settings. The new leader doesn't fully make the leadership transition, remains operational, managerial at the same time as trying to grasp the new leadership role.

In normal conditions, this can feel workable — even fine. The team is well-supported, things are held together, and the arrangement functions. But it carries a structural fragility that only becomes visible under pressure. When a surge hits, when an incident unfolds, when the organisational demands stack up, the person holding multiple roles simultaneously — operational, management, and clinical leadership — finds the load suddenly untenable. And the moment when the team most needs a stable, clear leadership presence is exactly the moment when it is hardest to provide one.

This tool is about making that functional shift — not through criticism of how things have been, but with a clear understanding of why things need to shift and a practical framework for moving differently. It is designed to support the transition with compassion and without blame: for the structural conditions that made full transition difficult, for the identity that formed around what was possible rather than what was intended, and for the genuine complexity of leading well in a system that doesn't always make leadership easy.

The identity is still forming. That is not a deficit. It is a starting point.

Where you are

The nature of the shift

Public health leadership is not a more senior version of what you did before. It is a different role — and understanding what that difference actually looks like in practice is the first step toward inhabiting it.

The central idea

The dance floor and the balcony

Ronald Heifetz and Marty Linsky at Harvard describe one of the most important disciplines of leadership using a simple image. Imagine a dance floor — full of movement, noise, immediate demand. When you are on the floor, you are in the action: responding, managing, solving what is right in front of you. Your attention is consumed by what is happening now.

Now imagine stepping off the floor and up to a balcony overlooking it. From there, you can see things that are completely invisible from below: the patterns across the whole floor, the dynamics between people, where flow is breaking down, where something is about to go wrong. You can ask questions that no one on the floor has the vantage point to ask.

In a responsive public health setting, the dance floor is the operational work — the responses, the workflows, the phone queues, the incident management. The balcony is the clinical leadership work: quality and standards oversight, pattern analysis across responses, process improvement, stakeholder relationships, education and professional development. Both are real. Both matter. But they require you to be in different positions — and you cannot fully occupy both at the same time.

The challenge for many people moving into clinical leadership is not a lack of willingness to lead. It is that the floor keeps pulling — through habit, through genuine operational need, through an identity that was built there. This tool is about developing the discipline to choose the balcony, and to understand why that choice is harder than it sounds.

Why the identity hasn't fully formed

When the role arrives without the scaffolding

Most leadership transitions in public health don't come with a clear period of preparation, a structured handover, or an explicit conversation about what the new role actually requires. The title changes. The pay band changes. The job description changes on paper. But the day-to-day often doesn't — because the operational need is still there, the team still needs covering, and the new leader is still the most capable operational person in the room.

When that happens, something predictable follows: the leadership identity doesn't fully form, because there is no space and no scaffolding for it to form in. The person holds the leadership title while continuing to do the operational work — not from resistance, but because nothing has made it sufficiently clear what "doing the leadership work" would actually look like in practice.

This is not a personal failing. It is a structural one — and it is extremely common in public health and other responsive operational settings. The result is a kind of identity limbo: you have been given the leadership role, but you don't yet fully inhabit it. The operational role is where you feel legitimate. The leadership role is where you feel uncertain.

🆔

Imposter syndrome in role transition

Research on leadership transitions describes a specific form of imposter syndrome that emerges when the title has arrived before the identity. The person performs confidence in the operational work — where they genuinely are competent — while feeling uncertain about whether they are "really" a leader. The operational role becomes the place to feel safe and capable. The leadership role feels like something they have been asked to perform, rather than something they are.

⚖️

Two things that fused over time

There was genuine structural necessity — the operational work needed doing and the workforce wasn't always there to do it without direct involvement. And there was identity comfort — the operational space was where capability felt real and grounded. These two things became fused over time, and when the structural necessity eventually eased, the identity anchor didn't automatically lift with it.

🔭

What is different now

Most people in this position reach moments when the structural conditions for stepping back genuinely shift — when management capacity arrives, when the team grows, when operational roles are filled by others. These moments don't automatically produce the leadership transition, but they create the opening for it. Recognising when that opening has arrived — and choosing to move through it — is the beginning of claiming the role in full.

What this role actually is

The substance of clinical leadership in public health

This is not a people management role — that sits with the nursing manager. This is a standards, quality, and systems leadership role. Its scope is distinct, significant, and genuinely demanding:

📊

Standards and quality

Holding and developing clinical and operational standards across the function. Noticing where practice is drifting, where quality is inconsistent, where what happens and what should happen have quietly diverged. Naming it, addressing it, and building the systems that sustain it — not as a one-off intervention, but as an ongoing discipline.

💡

Innovation and process improvement

Asking the questions that operational pressure prevents: is this the best way to do this? Where is the system creating unnecessary friction, duplication, or risk? What would a better process look like — and what would it take to build it? This is balcony work by definition. It cannot be done from the floor.

🌐

Stakeholder management and system navigation

Building and maintaining the relationships — with other parts of the health system, with partner organisations, with commissioning and governance structures — that allow the function to operate effectively and advocate for what it needs. This is leadership work that is invisible when it isn't done and enormously valuable when it is.

🎓

Education and professional development

Supporting the clinical and professional growth of the team — not through people management (that belongs to the nursing manager) but through the clinical leadership functions: setting learning expectations, creating opportunities for development, modelling the standard of practice and professional engagement you want to see.

The evidence base

What this is grounded in

Adaptive Leadership — Heifetz & Linsky (Harvard)

The dance floor and balcony framework: the discipline of moving between immersive operational response and reflective strategic leadership. The most important leadership skill is knowing which position is needed and choosing it deliberately rather than being pulled by habit or anxiety.

Psychological safety — Amy Edmondson (Harvard)

Teams in which people feel safe to make decisions, speak up, and handle complexity outperform those in which they don't. Leaders who remain operational inadvertently signal that the team isn't trusted to manage without them — and the team learns not to try.

Identity and role transition research

When professional identity fuses with operational function — especially when the transition was under-supported — stepping back feels like loss rather than growth. Understanding this psychologically is what makes the transition sustainable rather than something to be endured indefinitely.

Two positions

The operational floor and the leadership balcony

In responsive public health work, both positions are real and both have value. The question is not which one matters — it's which one is yours now, and whether you're choosing it or being pulled there by habit.

What each position offers

Two different kinds of work

Neither position is permanently right. The leadership role requires moving between them with intention — but the discipline is to make the balcony your default, and the floor a deliberate, chosen exception.

On the operational floor

You are in the response — managing workflows, handling calls, running the ops lead function.

Feedback is immediate. Competence is visible. The outcome is clear.

You feel useful and needed in a way that is tangible and certain.

This is where your identity has been anchored.

On the leadership balcony

You can see patterns across responses that are invisible to those managing them.

You can observe how the team is developing professionally and where the educational or practice gaps are.

You can ask the questions that shift the team's thinking rather than just solving the immediate problem.

You are building something that outlasts any single response or shift.

The cost of staying on the floor

What remains unseen and undone

👁

The system patterns go unanalysed

When you are in the response, you are solving the incident in front of you. The question of why the same type of incident keeps occurring — what the system is producing that it shouldn't — belongs on the balcony. It doesn't get asked from the floor.

🔄

The team's development stalls

The team is often growing — but the leader's growth into the leadership role is constrained by continued operational involvement. Leadership identity and capability form through the practice of leadership. When that practice keeps being displaced by operational demands, the identity doesn't fully consolidate — and the leadership function remains underdeveloped precisely where it is most needed.

⚡

You exhaust yourself on the wrong work

Holding three roles — operational, management, and clinical leadership — is sustainable under normal conditions and invisible as a risk until pressure hits. When it does, the compounding load becomes suddenly unmanageable, and the stability that the team relies on fractures at exactly the moment they need it most. This is not a failure of individual resilience. It is a structural problem with a structural solution.

🌱

The leadership identity can't form

You cannot build confidence in a leadership identity while remaining primarily in an operational role. The identity forms through the practice — through the questions asked, the patterns noticed, the development work done. If that work isn't happening, the identity stays uncertain.

Moving between them

When to step down — and when to return

The balcony is your default position now. The floor is a deliberate, chosen exception — not a habit, not a comfort retreat, and not a way of showing solidarity with a team under pressure.

There are genuine moments to step onto the floor: a situation that exceeds the team's current capability, a genuine safety concern that is unmanaged, a teaching moment where your presence alongside someone will accelerate their development. These are floor moments — chosen, purposeful, and temporary.

The discipline is not just knowing when to step down. It is developing the habit of stepping back up — and doing so quickly, before the floor reclaims you by familiarity.

Understanding the pull

Why stepping back is hard

The pull back to operational involvement isn't a flaw. It's a deeply human response to uncertainty, identity, and the genuine desire to be useful. Understanding it is how you begin to work with it rather than against it.

What drives the pull

Six forces that bring you back to the floor

🆔

Identity — "This is who I am"

When your professional identity is built around operational excellence, stepping back from it can feel like stepping away from yourself. Researchers call this "role absorption" — when the function becomes the self. The floor is where you know who you are.

🛡

Control — "If I don't do it, it won't be done right"

Years of high standards and deep expertise make it genuinely difficult to trust that others will reach the same bar. This is not arrogance — it's often accurate in the short term. But in the long term, it prevents the team from ever reaching that bar.

⚡

Speed — "It's faster if I just do it"

In the short term, this is often true. It is faster to fix something yourself than to coach someone through fixing it. But the short-term efficiency creates long-term dependency — and the same problem returns, indefinitely, requiring your intervention each time.

💡

Competence certainty — "I know how to do this"

Operational work provides immediate, visible feedback. You do something, and you can see whether it worked. Leadership work is slower, less visible, and harder to measure. The floor offers certainty; the balcony offers ambiguity. Of course the floor is attractive.

🤝

Belonging — "I want to stay one of the team"

There is something genuinely warm about being embedded with your colleagues — working alongside them, sharing the same pressures. Stepping to the balcony can feel like separating yourself. But a leader who remains peer-embedded cannot hold the team safely.

🆘

Anxiety — "Something will go wrong if I step back"

In high-stakes clinical environments, the stakes are real. The fear of harm drives vigilance. But there is a difference between appropriate oversight and anxious involvement. The clinical leader's job is to build a team whose standard of care does not depend on their constant presence.

A reframe worth sitting with

The pull is information

When you feel the pull to step in — notice it before acting on it. Ask: what is this pull protecting? Is it protecting a standard, a response quality, a genuine gap? Or is it protecting a familiar sense of self, a feeling of certainty, a discomfort with the space you leave?

Not every pull needs to be resisted. Some of them are correct — there are genuine moments to step down. But the practice begins with the pause: noticing the pull, naming it, and then choosing your response rather than following the impulse automatically.

A different kind of attention

The questions that belong to you now

Clinical leadership involves a distinct set of questions — ones that only someone in your position can ask. These are the balcony questions. They are different from the operational questions, and learning to notice the difference is a core part of the role.

Floor questions vs balcony questions

Knowing which question is yours

Floor questions are not wrong — they are necessary. But they belong to the nursing manager and the operational team. The balcony questions are the ones only you, in your position, are placed to ask.

Floor questions (nursing manager / ops team)

How do we manage this workload today?

Who is covering which role this shift?

What is the fastest way to resolve this response?

What are today's priorities for the team?

Balcony questions (yours)

Where is our clinical practice diverging from our standard?

What pattern am I seeing across these responses or incidents?

What process improvement would make the biggest difference?

What do our stakeholders need from us that we aren't providing?

What does this team need to learn — and how do I create that?

Balcony questions in practice

What to ask, and when

📊

About quality and standards

Where is practice genuinely meeting the standard — and where is it meeting it on paper but not in reality? What patterns am I seeing across our responses, our near-misses, our complaints? Is there a systemic thread I haven't yet named? What would a quality review of our work actually show?

💡

About process and innovation

Where is the system creating unnecessary friction — for the team, for the people we serve, for our partners? What would a better workflow look like? Where are we doing things a certain way because that's how they've always been done — and is that still the right reason?

🌐

About the system we operate in

What do our key stakeholders need from us, and are we providing it? Where are the relationships that need building or repairing? What is happening in the broader health system that we should be responding to — or influencing? Who needs to know what we are doing and why?

🎓

About education and professional development

What is the clinical and professional learning this team needs — individually and collectively? Where are the gaps in knowledge or practice that I can see from the balcony but that are invisible from the floor? What education would strengthen the standard we're holding? How can I support the nursing manager's people development work with clinical insight?

🔭

About your own leadership

Am I spending time on what only I can do? What leadership work has been deferred for the next quiet period that never arrives? Where is my relationship with the nursing manager genuinely working — and where does it need clarifying? What does the team need from this role that it isn't currently getting?

A practice, not a test

You won't have good answers to all of these questions all of the time. The point isn't the answers — it's the habit of asking. A public health leader who regularly asks these questions, even imperfectly, is doing the work. One who rarely asks them — because the operational floor is always too demanding — is not doing the leadership role, regardless of how hard they are working.

The hardest practice

Role discipline — staying off the floor

Understanding the shift conceptually is one thing. The practice is something else. Role discipline is the set of specific, concrete commitments that keep you on the balcony when every instinct is pulling you down. It is not passive. It requires active, daily choices.

What role discipline means in practice

The specific things to stop

These are the behaviours that feel helpful, collegial, or urgent — and that quietly undermine the transition every time they happen.

📋

Rostering yourself into operational roles

Even when the roster is tight. Even when it feels like the responsible thing to do. Every time you appear on the operational roster, you signal to the team, to the organisation, and to yourself that you are still an operational resource. That signal is very hard to undo. Your name on the roster is not a small thing.

📞

Answering the phones and managing workflows

In a busy response environment, the phone rings and someone needs to pick it up. That person should not be you. Not because the call doesn't matter — it does — but because every time you manage a workflow or take a call, you step out of the position from which you can see the whole system. And the team learns that you will step in, so they wait for you to.

🤝

Taking the ops lead role to show you're a team player

This one is particularly difficult because the impulse behind it is genuine — you care about your team, you see they're under pressure, and stepping in feels like the right expression of solidarity. But a leader who takes the ops lead role to ease pressure is removing themselves from their actual function and teaching the team that solidarity means sharing the operational load, rather than building a system that manages it differently.

🔄

Stepping in to reduce the team's pressure by absorbing it yourself

The pressure the team is under is real. The impulse to relieve it is humane. But absorbing operational pressure into yourself is not a sustainable leadership strategy — and it prevents the more important work of addressing why the pressure exists in the first place. That second work requires the balcony. You cannot do it from the floor.

What to do instead

The balcony response to operational pressure

Be visibly present, not operationally involved. Move through the space. Ask targeted questions. "What's the priority right now?" "What do you need?" "What would make the next hour more manageable?" This signals care and attention without removing you from the overview position.

Guide those who hold the operational roles rather than replacing them. Your job when things are busy is to support the ops lead, not to become the ops lead. "What's your read on the situation? What would help you right now?" keeps them in their role and you in yours.

Name what you're seeing, not doing. "I can see this is a heavy period. I'm watching how we're managing it and I'll debrief with you afterward." This is balcony presence — you are paying attention, you are engaged, and you are not on the floor.

Use the pressure as information. A period of high operational demand is your richest source of balcony data. What is the system struggling to manage? Where are the process weaknesses? Where is the standard holding and where is it drifting? What does this period reveal about what needs to change structurally or educationally? None of these questions can be answered if you are answering phones.

Working with the nursing manager — not instead of them

A relationship to invest in deliberately

Where a management role exists alongside yours — whether that is a nursing manager, an operations manager, or a team leader — the opportunity is to utilise it fully rather than sitting alongside it without full effect. This means actively supporting the conditions in which that role can function with real authority and real confidence, rather than unconsciously remaining the go-to for everything that role should hold.

This means being clear about the distinction between what belongs to them and what belongs to you. People management, rostering, day-to-day operational oversight — theirs. Standards, quality, clinical practice, innovation, system navigation — yours. Where these intersect, the question is how you work together, not which of you takes over.

Back their decisions visibly. When the nursing manager makes a decision — about staffing, about a staff situation, about operational priorities — your role is to support that publicly, not to revisit it. If you have a concern, raise it with them directly, not by stepping around them.

Offer clinical insight, not operational override. When a people management or operational situation has a clinical dimension, your role is to provide the clinical perspective — not to take over the management of it. "Here's what I'm seeing clinically — how does that change your thinking?" keeps the authority where it belongs.

Make the role boundary explicit and agreed. Have a direct conversation about where each role starts and stops — not as a one-off, but as an ongoing calibration. "What feels unclear between our roles right now?" is a question worth asking regularly, especially as the team's structure evolves.

Let them bring you problems rather than you bringing them solutions. If you are consistently arriving with solutions to operational problems, you are still, functionally, the operational lead. If they are arriving to you with problems and you are helping them think through solutions, you are a clinical leadership resource — which is the right relationship.

The honest difficulty

What this will feel like

Role discipline will feel wrong before it feels right. Standing back when things are busy will feel like abandonment. Watching someone handle something more slowly than you would will feel frustrating. Not picking up the phone will feel irresponsible. The team may express that they miss having you more present operationally — and that will feel like evidence that stepping back was a mistake.

None of these feelings are evidence that stepping back was wrong. They are the feelings of a new identity forming under uncomfortable conditions. The discomfort is the transition. It does not mean you are doing it incorrectly.

What changes is not the feeling — at least not immediately. What changes is what you do with the feeling. You notice it, name it, and hold the role discipline anyway.

Real moments

When the pull is strongest

These are the specific moments in public health leadership when the operational floor exerts its hardest pull. Each one explores what's driving it, what it costs, and what staying on the balcony looks like instead.

You walk past something that needs doing and no one is doing it

A task is clearly outstanding. You could fix it in two minutes.

›

The pull: to fix it. It would take you two minutes, it needs doing, and walking past it feels like abdication.

When you fix it without addressing why it wasn't done, the pattern continues. The team learns that outstanding tasks resolve themselves. You've solved the symptom and left the cause intact — and you'll be back here tomorrow.

Balcony response: Note it. Then ask the relevant person: "I noticed X wasn't done — what was happening there?" You're gathering information, not assigning blame. The answer tells you something about workload, capability, or prioritisation — all balcony questions.

If it genuinely can't wait: Ask someone to do it rather than doing it yourself. "Can you get to X before the end of the hour?" This stays in your role while still moving things forward.

The longer pattern: If this keeps happening, that's your real work — understanding why, and what needs to change structurally. That's a balcony conversation, not a floor fix.

A staff member comes to you with a problem they should be able to solve

Someone brings you a clinical or operational question that's within their scope and competence.

›

The pull: to answer it. You know the answer. It's quicker. They're asking.

Every time you answer a question someone could have worked through, you confirm their belief that they need you for this. The queue of questions grows. Your day fills with problems that belong elsewhere. And the person asking stops developing the judgment they need.

Balcony response: "What do you think?" — asked genuinely, not as a test. Then listen. You often find they already know the answer and needed permission, not information. Your role was to create the space for them to trust their own judgment.

If they genuinely don't know: Walk them through your thinking rather than giving them your conclusion. "Here's how I'd approach this — what factors would you weigh?" builds their decision-making framework, not dependence on yours.

The pattern to notice: If the same person keeps bringing you solvable problems, that's a development conversation — about building their confidence and authority, not about the individual questions.

Things are getting busy and your instinct is to pitch in

The team is under pressure. Stepping in feels like the right thing to do.

›

The pull: solidarity, usefulness, care for your team. And if you don't help, who will?

When you move to the floor under pressure, you lose the oversight function at exactly the moment when oversight matters most. Busy periods are when errors cluster, when communication breaks down, when patterns become visible — to someone who is watching.

Balcony response during pressure: Move through the space, visible and calm. Ask short targeted questions: "What do you need right now?" "What's the biggest priority?" This signals presence and care without removing you from the overview position.

There are genuine moments to step in: A patient safety issue that is unmanaged. A team member who is genuinely out of their depth. A situation that exceeds the team's current capacity. These are floor moments — chosen, not habitual.

After the pressure passes: Reflect with the team. "That was a hard stretch — what would have made it more manageable?" This is balcony work that directly serves the next difficult period.

Someone does something differently than you would have

A team member handles a situation in a way that isn't how you'd have done it — but it's within safe limits.

›

The pull: to step in and redirect. To make sure it's done the right way. Your way.

When you consistently redirect to your way, you communicate that there is only one right approach — yours. This stifles clinical judgment, creates anxiety, and produces a team of people who wait for your instruction rather than exercising their own expertise.

The question to ask yourself first: Is this a safety issue? Is it a quality issue? Or is it just not how I would have done it? The first two warrant input. The third rarely does.

If it's worth discussing: Do it afterward, not during. "How did you think through that?" is better than "you should have done X." The goal is understanding their reasoning, not correcting it toward yours.

Notice what it tells you: Multiple people doing things differently in ways that concern you suggests a standards conversation with the whole team — not individual corrections that leave the pattern intact.

The nursing manager brings you an operational problem to solve

A people management or operational situation lands with you — and you have a clear view of what should be done.

›

The pull: to solve it. You know the situation, you have the experience, and the nursing manager is asking. It would be faster and it would relieve their stress.

Every time you solve an operational or people management problem that belongs to the nursing manager, you signal — however unintentionally — that the role isn't fully theirs. Over time they bring you problems rather than developing their own judgment about them. The boundary between your roles becomes blurred, and your capacity for the clinical leadership work narrows further.

Offer thinking, not decisions. "Here's how I'd think about this — what's your read?" keeps the decision with them and develops their judgment. You are a resource, not a substitute.

Name the boundary gently when it helps. "That sits in your domain more than mine — I think you've got what you need to call this." This is support, not abandonment. It backs their authority explicitly.

If there's a genuine clinical dimension: Provide that specifically. "Clinically, I'd be thinking about X — does that change what you're planning?" This is your contribution — not the operational management of it.

You see a team member making slow progress on a delegated task

Someone is working on something you could do faster — and time matters.

›

The pull: to take it back. You're watching something that could be done in half the time, and the clock is moving.

Taking a task back teaches the person that slow progress results in removal of responsibility. They learn to either rush (at the cost of quality) or to flag problems earlier than they otherwise would — neither of which builds the capability you need them to develop.

First question: Is the pace genuinely creating a risk — to a response quality or a deadline that matters? Or is it just slower than you'd be? Only the first warrants intervention.

If you need to intervene: Do it alongside, not by taking over. "Let me work through this with you" keeps them in the task and makes the thinking visible. It's a teaching moment, not a rescue.

If the pace is just slow: Let it be. Debrief afterward. "How did you feel about your pace on that?" opens the conversation without judgment and gives them the chance to reflect on what they'd do differently.

The team is asking you to be more present on the floor

Staff are expressing that they want you more involved, more hands-on, more "one of us."

›

The pull: to respond to what they're asking for — to be more present operationally, to meet their expressed need.

What the team asks for and what the team needs are not always the same thing. A team asking for more operational presence from their leader may actually be expressing anxiety, under-confidence, or a lack of clarity about their own authority — none of which is solved by the leader doing more floor work.

Listen to the feeling, not the request: "I hear that you want me more involved — can you tell me more about what's feeling unsupported?" The answer usually reveals what is actually needed, which is rarely more operational input from you.

What they may actually need: Clearer authority ("who decides X when you're not here?"), more confidence ("you're ready for this — I trust your judgment"), or more connection ("let's have a proper team check-in each week"). These are balcony interventions that address the real need.

Be honest: "My role has changed, and I know that's an adjustment for all of us. My job now is to support you to be excellent — not to do what you do alongside you. Let's talk about what support actually looks like from where I'm standing now."
